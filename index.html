For HR Professionals & Managers

Before the Resume Work Starts

The moments immediately following separation set the tone for everything that follows. These tools help you show up with both honesty and dignity — and ask better questions of your outplacement providers.

Tool 01

The First Conversation Guide

What to say — and what to avoid — in the notification meeting and the day-two follow-up.

↓ PDF

Most managers have never been taught how to do this well. The goal is clarity and dignity — not comfort, efficiency, or closure on your timeline.

"This conversation won't feel complete. It shouldn't. Your job isn't to make this okay — it's to be honest and present."

The Notification Conversation — What Helps

  • Be direct. State clearly that the position is being eliminated before explaining why.
  • Acknowledge the weight of the moment before moving to logistics.
  • Ask: "What questions can I answer for you right now?" — then actually wait.
  • Give them time. Silence is not a problem to be solved.
  • Be clear about what you know and honest about what you don't.

What Makes It Worse

  • "You'll land on your feet." It minimizes what's happening right now.
  • "At least you have severance." Same problem.
  • Rushing to next steps before the person has absorbed the news.
  • Over-explaining the business rationale to manage your own discomfort.
  • "We can still be connected on LinkedIn." Not the moment.

The Day-Two Follow-Up

A brief check-in the day after notification is more valuable than it might seem. These three work:

"I wanted to check in — is there anything that would be helpful from me in the next few days?"

"What's feeling most uncertain right now?"

"I want to be honest with you about what I know and what I'm still finding out."

Tool 02

Vendor Evaluation Guide

Seven questions that reveal whether an outplacement provider understands the full human transition — not just the job search.

↓ PDF

Most vendors will tell you about placement rates, coach credentials, and platform features. These questions go deeper. Listen for what they say — and what they don't.

  • 01

    What does your program look like in the first two weeks, before resume work begins?

    Listen for: acknowledgment of emotional transition, identity work, readiness assessment. Red flag: immediate pivot to resume and LinkedIn optimization.

  • 02

    How do you assess where someone is emotionally before prescribing next steps?

    Listen for: intake conversations, coach discretion, flexibility. Red flag: one-size-fits-all timelines for everyone.

  • 03

    What do you do when a client has strong credentials but isn't making progress?

    Listen for: identity exploration, root-cause thinking. Red flag: "We increase the volume of applications."

  • 04

    How do you measure success beyond placement rate and time-to-employment?

    Listen for: confidence metrics, client-reported readiness, quality-of-fit. Red flag: blank stare.

  • 05

    What does peer or cohort support look like in your model?

    Listen for: facilitated peer groups, shared experience structures. Red flag: "Our platform has discussion boards."

  • 06

    What training do your coaches have in identity transition — not just job-search tactics?

  • 07

    Can you describe what you do for clients who are grieving the future they thought they were building?

    A vendor who doesn't understand the question probably can't answer it.

For Career Coaches & Practitioners

When the Tools Aren't the Problem

Sometimes a client has everything they need to search — and still isn't moving. These questions are for those conversations. They focus on identity and meaning, not tactics.

Tool 03

The Stuck Client Conversation

Twelve questions for use when a client is prepared but not progressing — organized around the real work of identity transition.

↓ PDF

Use these selectively, not as a script. The goal is to surface what's actually getting in the way — which is rarely a skills gap or a resume problem.

On Meaning

  • 01

    What did that role mean to you — beyond the job description?

  • 02

    What are you afraid this job loss says about you?

  • 03

    What part of your professional identity feels most uncertain right now?

On Identity

  • 04

    Who were you before this role? What parts of that person are still here?

  • 05

    When you imagine introducing yourself professionally — without a title — what do you say?

  • 06

    What have you learned about yourself in this period that you want to carry forward?

On Readiness

  • 07

    Is there a version of "next" that excites you — even quietly or partially?

  • 08

    What would you have to believe about yourself to take the next step?

  • 09

    What does "feeling like yourself again" look like to you?

On Movement

  • 10

    What are you protecting by staying still?

  • 11

    What's the smallest step that would feel honest rather than performative?

  • 12

    What would make it okay to not have this figured out yet?

"The goal of these questions isn't resolution — it's to help the client locate where they actually are, so the work you do together can meet them there."

For Individuals in Transition

Before You Open LinkedIn

Job searching before you're ready is exhausting and often counterproductive. These two tools are for the days before the search begins — to help you understand where you are and what you're carrying.

Tool 04

Before the Resume: A Reflection Guide

Ten questions for the first week or two — about what the last role meant, not about your next one.

↓ PDF

Set aside 30–60 minutes. Find somewhere quiet. Write your answers — even roughly — rather than just thinking through them. Writing slows things down in a useful way.

  • 01

    What did this role mean to you?

  • 02

    What will you miss about it?

  • 03

    What are you relieved to leave behind?

  • 04

    What did you learn about yourself while in this role?

  • 05

    What parts of the work energized you? What drained you?

  • 06

    What do you want to carry forward into the next chapter?

  • 07

    Who were you before this role? What parts of that person are still true?

  • 08

    What would you want people to say about your work — not your title?

  • 09

    If you could design the next role with no constraints, what would matter most?

  • 10

    What does "doing well" look like to you — not as a professional, but as a person?

Tool 05

Transition Readiness Self-Assessment

A short, honest gauge of where you are — not where you think you should be.

↓ PDF

Answer these as honestly as you can. This isn't a test — it's a map. Knowing where you are helps you use the time and support available to you well.

  • 01

    When you imagine starting a job search this week, what's your first feeling?

    Relief and readiness are different from dread, numbness, or going through the motions.

  • 02

    Can you describe what you're looking for — not just in a role, but in how you want to work and feel?

    If the answer is "I just need a job," that's worth sitting with.

  • 03

    Do you feel like the same person who held your last role, or is something still shifting?

  • 04

    When you think about telling your professional story, does it feel true?

    Or does it feel like something you're performing?

  • 05

    On a scale of 1–10, how much energy do you have for the sustained work of job searching?

    Below a 6 or 7 isn't failure — it's information.

  • 06

    Are you staying busy to avoid feeling something?

If most of your answers point to uncertainty, numbness, or avoidance — you may still be in the transition gap. That's not a problem. It's information. The gap is where the real preparation happens.

About This Kit

This practitioner kit was developed as a companion to the article "Outplacement Is a Human Problem Before It Is a Hiring Problem," published in HRMOutlook. It is designed for use the same day — no training required, no platform to log into.

The resources here are grounded in years of work with adult learners, career changers, and professionals navigating workforce transition. They are informed by research on professional identity — particularly Herminia Ibarra's work on how people navigate career change.

These tools may be shared freely with individuals in transition and used by practitioners in their work. If you adapt them for an organizational program, attribution is appreciated.
